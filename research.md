As a computational social scientist trained in physics, I am driven by the application of mathematical models to better understand the societies we inhabit. The expansion of computational power, the availability of large-scale behavioral data, and the development of tools such as Large Language Models (LLMs) and other forms of Artificial Intelligence have transformed our ability to study social systems with unprecedented resolution. My research leverages these advances to examine how collective structures emerge, evolve, and influence human behavior across scientific communities, online platforms, and urban environments.

---

# Science of science

Science is increasingly a collective endeavor. Researchers collaborate to reach intellectual shores that would be inaccessible in isolation. Yet, despite the idealized image of science as a purely rational enterprise, scholars are also social beings, and collaboration patterns are deeply shaped by social norms, institutional structures, and historical inequalities.

My work in the science of science investigates how formal and informal collaboration networks influence the expansion of scientific domains, how early-career environments shape long-term trajectories, and how structural inequalities affect access, visibility, and impact within academia. By combining network analysis, statistical physics models, and large-scale bibliometric data, I aim to uncover the mechanisms through which scientific knowledge grows and who benefits from that growth.

# Online collective organization

Online platforms have become central arenas for information exchange and collective action. They allow individuals to connect instantly with millions of others, enabling rapid mobilization, grassroots organization, and new forms of political participation. At the same time, these same structural features facilitate misinformation cascades, coordinated harassment campaigns, and the strategic manipulation of democratic processes.

My research examines how network structure and information dynamics interact to shape collective behavior online. Using network science and LLM-based content analysis, I study the growth patterns of coordinated communities, the diffusion pathways of political framing, and the subtle ways in which narratives travel through seemingly apolitical spaces to reach broader audiences.

# Human–AI Interaction and the Emerging Information Ecosystem

Societies have historically relied on journalism, institutions, and social networks to mediate access to information. The rapid integration of generative AI systems into communication platforms is reshaping this ecosystem in profound ways.

I investigate how large language models participate in information production and consumption: how they influence framing, persuasion, and public discourse; how users adapt their communicative strategies in response to AI systems; and how these models may alter the balance between organic and synthetic information flows. This line of work combines computational experimentation with network-level analysis to understand how AI systems integrate into — and potentially transform — collective communication dynamics.

---

# Urban Mobility

Cities represent one of the most complex forms of human organization. From small villages to global metropolises, urban spaces concentrate economic exchange, social interaction, and cultural production. Patterns of mobility within cities reveal how people navigate opportunities, constraints, and inequalities embedded in physical space.

In this area, I develop statistically grounded models to study human mobility patterns and urban dynamics. By combining mobility data with network-based approaches, I explore how movement flows shape social connectivity, economic activity, and the diffusion of behaviors across neighborhoods. Often, simple and interpretable models can capture essential features of urban behavior, offering insight into how cities function as dynamic social systems.

<div class="research-intro">

As a Computational Social Scientist trained in physics, I apply mathematical and computational models to better understand the societies we live in. The expansion of large-scale behavioral data and the development of tools such as Large Language Models (LLMs) have transformed our ability to study collective dynamics. My work combines statistical physics, network science, and artificial intelligence to generate empirically grounded insights into how social systems organize, evolve, and respond to technological change.

</div>

<!-- Science of Science -->
<div class="research-section">

  <div class="research-text">
    <h2>Science of Science</h2>
    <p> Science is increasingly a collective endeavor. Researchers collaborate to reach intellectual shores that would be inaccessible in isolation. Yet, despite the idealized image of science as a purely rational enterprise, scholars are also social beings, and collaboration patterns are deeply shaped by social norms, institutional structures, and historical inequalities.
    </p>

    <p> My work in the science of science investigates how formal and informal collaboration networks influence the expansion of scientific domains, how early-career environments shape long-term trajectories, and how structural inequalities affect access, visibility, and impact within academia. By combining network analysis, statistical physics models, and large-scale bibliometric data, I aim to uncover the mechanisms through which scientific knowledge grows and who benefits from that growth.
    </p>

<!-- Online collective organization -->
<div class="research-section">

  <div class="research-text">
    <h2>Online collective organization</h2>
    <p> Online platforms have become central arenas for information exchange and collective action. They allow individuals to connect instantly with millions of others, enabling rapid mobilization, grassroots organization, and new forms of political participation. At the same time, these same structural features facilitate misinformation cascades, coordinated harassment campaigns, and the strategic manipulation of democratic processes.
    </p>

    <p> My research examines how network structure and information dynamics interact to shape collective behavior online. Using network science and LLM-based content analysis, I study the growth patterns of coordinated communities, the diffusion pathways of political framing, and the subtle ways in which narratives travel through seemingly apolitical spaces to reach broader audiences.
    </p>

<div class="research-section">

  <div class="research-text">
    <h2>Human–AI Interaction and the Emerging Information Ecosystem</h2>
    <p> Generative AI systems are rapidly becoming intermediaries in how information is produced, framed, and consumed. Their integration into communication platforms reshapes public discourse and alters the balance between organic and synthetic information flows.
    </p>

    <p> I analyze how LLMs influence framing, persuasion, and exposure to diverse viewpoints, and how users adapt their behavior in AI-mediated environments. Beyond identifying risks such as polarization or manipulation, I explore how AI systems can be designed to strengthen the information ecosystem — supporting human productivity, facilitating constructive cross-ideological dialogue, and improving access to reliable information. The goal is to generate empirical insights that inform governance frameworks and the responsible deployment of AI in public communication.
    </p>

<!-- Urban Mobility -->
<div class="research-section">

  <div class="research-text">
    <h2>Urban Mobility</h2>
    <p> Cities represent one of the most complex forms of human organization. From small villages to global metropolises, urban spaces concentrate economic exchange, social interaction, and cultural production. Patterns of mobility within cities reveal how people navigate opportunities and constraints embedded in the physical space.
    </p>
    
    <p> Indeed, urban growth and spatial organization are not random processes. They reflect historical investment patterns, institutional decisions, and persistent structural inequalities that shape who has access to which neighborhoods, resources, and social networks. These spatial structures influence how individuals move through the city, the communities they can connect with, and ultimately their life outcomes.
    </p>

    <p> I develop statistically grounded models of human mobility to understand how spatial constraints and individual behavior interact to produce large-scale urban patterns. By linking mobility flows to social connectivity and economic opportunity, this work aims to inform more equitable urban planning and evidence-based infrastructure policy.
    </p>
